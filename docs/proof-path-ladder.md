# Sandy Chaos Proof-Path Ladder v1

## Purpose

This document defines the working ladder used to move Sandy Chaos surfaces from interesting signal to externally legible proof-bearing output.

It exists because claim-tier discipline alone (`defensible` / `plausible` / `speculative`, or `F/C/E/S`) is not enough to answer a different operational question:

> Which surface should receive pressure today if the goal is to make the program more scientifically undeniable?

The ladder complements, rather than replaces:
- `FOUNDATIONS.md` claim and marker discipline
- `docs/theory-implementation-matrix.md` traceability
- `spine/concepts/*.yaml` concept state tracking
- `spine/pressure/*` pressure-event records
- Ygg baton / active-work state in the control plane

---

## Core rule

Every non-trivial Sandy Chaos surface should be placeable on exactly one proof-path level.

For each surface, the operator should be able to answer:
1. what level it is currently on,
2. what the next level is,
3. what smallest concrete move would advance it one level,
4. what failure condition would block or weaken that move.

If a surface cannot answer those questions, it is not yet governing work clearly enough.

---

## Level 1 — Signal Surface

**What it is**
A recurring pattern, anomaly, intuition, or structural pressure worth attention.

**Entry condition**
- there is more than aesthetic interest
- at least one phenomenon, question, or pattern can be named cleanly

**Exit condition**
- rewritten as a bounded claim with scope, assumptions, and failure conditions

**Common failure mode**
- staying broad, poetic, or cosmological
- treating resonance as evidence

---

## Level 2 — Structured Claim

**What it is**
A mechanism claim stated tightly enough to be attacked.

**Entry condition**
- explicit proposition
- scope and assumptions named
- claim tier declared
- at least one meaningful failure condition stated

**Exit condition**
- there exists a formal schema, computational representation, benchmark design, or operational artifact that instantiates the claim

**Common failure mode**
- persuasive prose with no inspectable object
- strong language substituting for operational structure

---

## Level 3 — Formalized Artifact

**What it is**
The claim has become an inspectable object: schema, model, algorithm sketch, simulation scaffold, benchmark spec, dataset protocol, or operational representation.

**Entry condition**
- another operator could inspect, load, or run the representation in some bounded form

**Exit condition**
- the artifact supports a discriminating comparison, test, ablation, or prediction rather than only internal demonstration

**Common failure mode**
- elegant scaffolding that proves internal consistency but not external relevance
- confusing “artifact exists” with “artifact matters”

---

## Level 4 — Discriminating Test

**What it is**
A test, benchmark, prediction, or comparison that can separate the Sandy Chaos mechanism from weaker alternatives.

**Entry condition**
- evaluation target is explicit
- at least one baseline or counterfactual exists
- measurable criterion is stated
- failure condition is stated in advance

**Exit condition**
- at least one completed result exists, or a clearly executable protocol is ready, that would produce legible evidence for or against the claim

**Common failure mode**
- pseudo-testing that only confirms the artifact runs
- comparisons that do not actually distinguish the mechanism from a simpler baseline

---

## Level 5 — External-Legible Result

**What it is**
A result that a skeptical outsider can parse without inheriting the full local worldview.

**Entry condition**
A compact proof object exists with:
- claim
- artifact
- method
- result or evaluation protocol
- limitations
- why it matters

**Exit condition**
- publishable or shareable result slice exists: note, demo, report, benchmark packet, or reproducible repo slice

**Common failure mode**
- valid internal work that remains illegible outside the project
- presentation that depends on too much local context or naming culture

---

## Ranking heuristic for daily work

When multiple surfaces compete for attention, rank them by:
1. **program leverage** — does it clarify or strengthen the whole research program?
2. **proof advancement** — does it move a surface up the ladder, not just sideways?
3. **dependency unlock** — does it unblock multiple later moves?
4. **external legibility** — does it help the work survive skeptical outside reading?
5. **today-feasibility** — can one real step be completed today?

Rule:
- no equal first priorities in the active frontier
- if two surfaces still tie, the ranking criteria are too soft and should be tightened before more work begins

---

## Relationship to claim tiers

Claim tiers answer:
- how strong is the claim today?

Proof-path levels answer:
- how mature is the work object today?

These are related but not identical.

Examples:
- a **plausible** claim can sit at Level 1, 2, 3, or 4
- a **defensible** subclaim may still be trapped at Level 3 if it has no outsider-legible test/result package
- a **speculative** idea can remain useful at Level 1 if it is clearly quarantined and not allowed to govern policy or promotion

Do not collapse claim strength and proof maturity into one label.

---

## Minimal operating procedure

For any active surface:
1. place it on one ladder level
2. name the next level
3. define one smallest move that would advance it one level
4. state the blocker or failure condition
5. rank it against competing surfaces
6. keep one active frontier only

---

## v1 scope note

This is a governance and prioritization instrument.
It does not itself prove scientific claims.
It exists to make sure Sandy Chaos pressure is pointed at the right proof surfaces, in the right order, with less lateral drift.
