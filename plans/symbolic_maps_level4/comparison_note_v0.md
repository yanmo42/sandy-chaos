## Provisional comparison by dimension

## 1. Cross-artifact consistency

### Arm A
Looks stronger.

Why:
- preserves more stable distinctions across the set
- repeatedly surfaces roles, operators, constraints, and failure conditions in comparable form
- makes it easier to see the shared lane logic between naming, specimen translation, and module architecture

### Arm B
Good enough for gist, weaker for stable comparison.

Why:
- themes recur, but vocabulary and structural emphasis drift more between artifacts
- comparisons rely more on reader interpretation than on packet structure

### Provisional judgment
**Arm A advantage: meaningful**

---

## 2. Round-trip reconstructability

### Arm A
Strong on all three artifacts.

### Arm B
Also surprisingly strong on the naming note and modules roadmap; weaker on Rimuru once the metaphor/model/mechanism boundary matters.

### Provisional judgment
**Advantage is mixed**

Interpretation:
- for contract-like or roadmap-like artifacts, Arm B may preserve enough of the core logic that Arm A does not dominate this dimension
- for specimen-style artifacts, Arm A appears more valuable because it preserves distinctions that looseness starts to blur

---

## 3. Completeness / structural coverage

### Arm A
Clearly stronger.

Why:
- captures not only the main gist, but also boundaries, failure conditions, reuse paths, and unresolved tensions in more stable form
- produces a packet that is easier to inspect and compare later

### Arm B
Adequate but less durable.

Why:
- captures the important ideas, but tends to fold structure back into summary prose
- does not naturally preserve reusable slots for later comparison or tooling

### Provisional judgment
**Arm A advantage: clear**

---

## 4. Downstream reuse

### Arm A
Clearly stronger.

Why:
- more useful for future schema design
- more useful for validator planning
- more useful for operator comparison
- more useful for deciding what a later loader/benchmark might consume

### Arm B
Useful for orientation and human reading, but weaker as a substrate for follow-on work.

### Provisional judgment
**Arm A advantage: clear**

---

## Main result

The first comparison suggests:

> **Invariant-guided extraction is probably earning its keep, but not because it always reconstructs the artifact better. It earns its keep because it produces more stable comparison structure, clearer boundary preservation, and better downstream reuse—especially once the artifact is more specimen-like or structurally mixed.**

That is a meaningful result.

---

## What this means for Symbolic Maps

### Defensible now
- the lane is not just empty style
- there is a real difference between a strong invariant-guided packet and a good loose symbolic reading
- that difference is strongest in structural reuse, comparison stability, and boundary preservation

### Plausible but unproven
- this advantage will hold across a larger and more heterogeneous artifact set
- the lane can support future validator/schema/loader work without collapsing into overformalized theater

### Not yet earned
- treating the lane as generally validated
- large buildout based only on this first benchmark pass
- assuming the current template is final or optimal

---

## Recommendation

### Keep Symbolic Maps alive as a live proof surface
But narrow the claim.

The strongest currently supportable claim is:

> **Narrative-invariant / symbolic-map extraction appears to produce a more reusable and comparison-friendly structure than a loose symbolic baseline, especially when artifacts mix symbolic richness with explicit boundary discipline.**

That is worth keeping.

### Do not overpromote yet
The lane should remain under pressure-testing until at least one more benchmark pass confirms that the advantage survives beyond this small three-artifact set.
